## Origin

Hack Club started in 2014 with a pretty simple but bold idea. Zach Latta, who was still a teenager at the time, noticed that most students were stuck watching tutorials or solving textbook problems without ever building anything real. Along with Jonathan Leung, he decided to flip that model completely.

Instead of "learn first, build later," Hack Club was built on "build first, learn along the way." They started with student-led coding clubs where teenagers weren't just learners, they were creators and leaders. There was no guarantee it would work, but it gave students something they weren't getting anywhere else: the chance to actually make things that exist.

## Early Days

In the beginning, Hack Club was small. Just a handful of schools, a few students, and a lot of experimentation. Clubs were messy, unstructured, and sometimes chaotic. People built random projects, many of which failed, but that failure was part of the process.

What made it different was that students weren't following a fixed curriculum. They were exploring, breaking things, fixing them, and learning in a way that felt real. Slowly, more students joined, more clubs started, and the idea began to spread beyond just a few locations.

## Growth

Over time, Hack Club expanded into a global network. Clubs started appearing in different cities and countries, each with its own style but the same core idea of building real things.

Students weren't just coding anymore. They were organizing hackathons, leading communities, contributing to open source, and experimenting with ideas they had never tried before. Hack Club became a place where teenagers could go from having no experience to actually shipping projects and leading initiatives.

Today, it has grown into a network of tens of thousands of teenagers across the world, with hundreds of active clubs and a constantly growing community.

## Culture

Hack Club's culture is what really defines it. It's not about perfection or competition in the usual sense. It's about building, sharing, failing, and trying again.

Projects break all the time. Ideas don't work. Things go wrong more often than they go right. But instead of stopping, people keep going. There's a strong sense of helping each other, where beginners ask questions and eventually become the ones answering them.

It's less about "learning a language" and more about becoming someone who can take an idea and turn it into something real.

## What Hack Club Looks Like Today

Today, Hack Club is a global community that's active both online and offline. Students are building websites, apps, hardware projects, open-source tools, and sometimes completely random experiments that still end up teaching them something valuable.

There's always something happening. Someone launching a project, someone organizing an event, someone asking for help, someone giving it. The community is constantly moving, and that energy is what keeps it alive.

## YSWS (You Ship, We Ship)

One of the most defining parts of Hack Club today is YSWS, which stands for "You Ship, We Ship."

The idea is simple: you build something real, and Hack Club supports you in taking it further by providing resources like hardware, domains, or other tools. Instead of giving resources first, it rewards action. You don't just sign up and get things, you actually have to create something.

This creates a strong incentive to build. People push themselves to finish projects, not just start them. And in the process, they learn more than they would by just consuming content.

YSWS represents what Hack Club has always been about: building first, learning by doing, and turning ideas into reality.

## The Real Impact

Most people don't remember Hack Club for specific lessons or tutorials. They remember the things they built, the events they organized, and the people they met.

It's the late nights fixing bugs, the first successful project, the chaos of running something for the first time, and the moment when you realize you can actually create things from scratch.

Hack Club started as a small idea in 2014, but it grew into something much bigger because it gave teenagers the space to try, fail, and keep going.
